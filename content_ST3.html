Hydropower: Energy from Flowing Water

Hydropower is the world's oldest and largest source of renewable electricity, accounting for approximately 16% of global electricity generation. By harnessing the natural movement of water through rivers, reservoirs, and tidal flows, hydropower plants generate clean, reliable energy that supports SDG 7 — ensuring access to affordable, reliable, sustainable, and modern energy for all. Unlike solar and wind, hydropower can generate electricity continuously, making it a vital backbone of clean energy grids worldwide.

How Hydropower Works

Hydropower plants convert the potential energy of water held at height into kinetic energy as it falls, and then into electrical energy using turbines and generators. Water flows from a reservoir or river through a penstock — a large pipe or channel — spinning turbine blades connected to a generator. The faster and greater the volume of water flow, the more electricity is produced.

Key Components

A conventional hydroelectric plant consists of a dam or weir to control water flow, a reservoir to store water, intake gates, a penstock channel, turbines, generators, and transmission equipment. Control systems regulate how much water flows through at any time, allowing operators to ramp electricity output up or down within seconds — a flexibility that makes hydropower uniquely valuable for balancing grids that increasingly rely on intermittent wind and solar power.

"Hydropower is the world's largest source of renewable electricity, providing 57% of all renewable power generation globally and acting as a critical balancing tool for modern energy grids." — International Hydropower Association, 2023

Types of Hydropower

Hydropower comes in several forms, ranging from massive dams to small run-of-river installations, making it adaptable to a wide variety of geographies and energy needs.

Conventional Reservoir Hydropower

The most common form, where a dam creates a large reservoir. Water is released in a controlled manner through turbines to generate electricity. These plants can store energy for weeks or months, providing reliable power on demand regardless of weather conditions. Examples include the Hoover Dam in the USA and the Three Gorges Dam in China — the world's largest power station of any kind.

Run-of-River Hydropower

Run-of-river plants divert a portion of a river's natural flow through turbines without creating a large reservoir. They have a much smaller environmental footprint than dam-based systems and are well suited to mountainous regions with fast-flowing rivers. Many small and medium run-of-river schemes operate across Scotland, Norway, and the Alps.

Pumped-Storage Hydropower

Pumped-storage plants act as giant rechargeable batteries for the electricity grid. During periods of low demand or excess renewable generation, water is pumped uphill to an upper reservoir. When demand peaks, water is released back through turbines to generate electricity rapidly. This technology is critical to integrating large amounts of solar and wind power into national grids.

Benefits of Hydropower

Hydropower offers a unique combination of reliability, longevity, and environmental benefits that distinguishes it from other renewable energy sources.

Environmental Impact

Once constructed, hydropower plants produce negligible direct greenhouse gas emissions during operation. Many plants operate for 50 to 100 years with minimal maintenance, providing decades of clean electricity from a single installation. Unlike solar panels or wind turbines, the core infrastructure of a hydro plant does not require periodic replacement, reducing its long-term material footprint significantly.

Reliability and Grid Stability

Hydropower's ability to respond instantly to changes in electricity demand makes it uniquely valuable. Countries like Norway generate nearly 90% of their electricity from hydropower and use the flexibility of their reservoirs to export electricity to neighbouring countries when demand is high. This dispatchable nature — the ability to generate on command — makes hydro an essential partner for variable renewables like wind and solar.

Economic and Social Benefits

Hydropower projects bring significant economic development to host regions, creating construction jobs, permanent operational roles, and improving infrastructure access. In many developing nations, small-scale hydropower provides first-time electricity access to rural communities that are far from national grids, enabling businesses, schools, and health clinics to operate reliably.

What Students Can Do

While building a dam isn't on the student to-do list, there are several meaningful ways to support hydropower and the broader clean energy transition.

1. Learn About Your Local Energy Mix

Find out what percentage of your region's electricity comes from hydropower and other renewables. Tools like the National Grid ESO's carbon intensity API (UK) show real-time data on where your electricity comes from. Understanding this helps you make informed choices and advocate for better policy.

2. Support Sustainable Hydropower Campaigns

Not all hydropower is created equal — large dam projects can displace communities and damage ecosystems. Support organisations that advocate for sustainable hydropower certification standards, such as the Hydropower Sustainability Council, which ensures projects meet environmental and social benchmarks.

3. Reduce Energy Waste During Peak Hours

Grid operators rely on hydro reservoirs to meet peak electricity demand. By shifting your heavy energy use — charging laptops, cooking, running washing machines — to off-peak hours, you reduce strain on the grid and help conserve reservoir levels for when they are needed most.

4. Explore Careers in Water and Energy

The hydropower sector needs engineers, environmental scientists, hydrologists, policy experts, and project managers. Many universities offer water resources and renewable energy modules. Internships with organisations like the Environment Agency or energy firms can provide direct experience in the sector.

The Global Picture

Hydropower currently provides over 4,300 terawatt-hours of electricity annually — more than all other renewable energy sources combined. China, Brazil, Canada, the USA, and Russia are the world's top hydro producers. Brazil generates over 60% of its national electricity from hydropower, while Paraguay's Itaipu Dam supplies nearly 90% of that country's entire electricity needs.

Despite its maturity, hydropower still has significant untapped potential. Africa and Asia both have large undeveloped hydro resources that could provide affordable clean electricity to hundreds of millions of people currently without reliable power access. The International Energy Agency projects that global hydropower capacity will grow by 17% by 2030, with much of this growth occurring in emerging economies. When combined with expanding solar and wind capacity, hydropower's storage and flexibility capabilities make it an irreplaceable pillar of the clean energy future.
